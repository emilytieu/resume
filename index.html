Wormholes and the Possibility of Time Travel
Unpublished Manuscript · General Relativity · March 2026 · First Author
By analyzing solutions to Einstein’s field equations, it is evident that spacetime admits various nontrivial geometries, including structures that connect two distant regions. Beginning with the Schwarzschild solution, the extension to the Einstein–Rosen bridge demonstrated how Einstein’s equations naturally permitted such connections. However, these early models were restricted as non-traversable, limiting their physical applicability. The later development of traversable wormhole models introduced metrics explicitly allowing passage, though at the cost of violating fundamental energy conditions. These spacetime geometries thereby lend themselves to structures called closed timelike curves, creating a model theoretically capable of time travel. Despite their mathematical validity, such solutions require unphysical conditions, presenting limitations of what is permitted by general relativity and what may actually be realized in nature.

Dynamic Systems Theory: An Improved Model of Cognitive Processing
Unpublished Manuscript · Computational Cognitive Science · April 2025 · First Author
Dynamical Systems Theory (DST) is a model of human cognitive processing via a system of nonlinear differential equations. Cognition is best detailed by the patterns mapped by these systems, allowing both a quantitative and qualitative understanding of real-time processing. Points on a DST model are plotted on a state space, a system of internal and external inputs over time that affect thoughts and behaviours. These points follow a trajectory, culminating at an attractor that is most influential to processing. On a model of neurotic behaviour, these attractors might include conflicting stressors and coping mechanisms that each cause variability in the resulting system. Moreover, applications of DST can be used for analyzing physical and mental development over time. In contrast to previous models of development, DST enables a continuous shift between stages of development, accounting for individual differences. Dynamic systems can also be examined for equilibrium patterns. As fluctuations in cognitive processes and the environment are imminent, it is important for humans to balance stability and flexibility mechanisms to appropriately adapt to changes they encounter. Consequently, DST entails inclusivity in individual differences, making it a better alternative to discrete models of cognition.

Beyond Dual-Process Theory: Religiosity, Cognition, and Rationalism
Unpublished Manuscript · Cognitive Science · April 2026 · First Author
The current study seeks to examine the role of analytic thinking within dual-process theory and its implications for religiosity, logical reasoning, and the Great Rationality Debate. The dual-process model distinguishes between intuitive and analytic processing, referred to as System 1 (S1) and System 2 (S2) respectively. Empirical evidence presents a consistently negative, albeit weak, correlation between analytic cognitive processing and religiosity, indicating that individuals who are more inclined to engage in S2 processing tend to be more skeptical of supernatural beliefs. This relationship is further scrutinized through the existence of belief bias, where S2 reasoning is thought to promote logical evaluation rather than reliance on prior beliefs. Dual-process theory has faced several critiques, however, as its lack of consideration for individual and cultural differences raises concern about its generalizability. To address these limitations, a new tripartite model is introduced, dividing S2 into a multilevel system. Dual-processing also plays a central role in sparking the rationality wars, where normative rationality of ideal behaviour is contrasted with adaptive, context-sensitive cognition. These perspectives challenge assumptions that religiosity necessarily reflects irrationality. Overall, while dual-processing remains an influential base for explaining human cognition, it must continue to undergo theoretical refinement to account for contextual and individual variability.

Maladaptive Behaviour Caused by Interpersonal Overdependence
Unpublished Manuscript · Behavioural Neuroscience · March 2025 · First Author
Human interdependence, while inherently a core component of our everyday lives, may prove to cause unsteady development. As a consequence of the normative availability of physical and financial resources as we age, these physical needs thereby lend themselves to individual struggles for a balance between autonomy and dependency (Fiori, et al., 2008). Furthermore, dependent individuals are often described to be anxious, conscientious, and clingy, particularly when required to act autonomously (Bornstein, 2012). This concept has since been referred to as interpersonal dependency, which encompasses the complexity of human emotions and behaviours towards the desire to associate closely with other people (Hirschfield, et al., 1977). As opposed to other attributes, Hirschfield, et al. (1977) discovered that lack of self-confidence is almost exclusive to dependency. Hutchins and Raith (2014) extend these tendencies to the development of maladaptive behaviours, where those who are overly dependent often struggle to adjust appropriately to their environment. As such, the current study aims to unveil the extent to which the human tendency to overly depend on one another induces maladjustment. By introducing destructive overdependence, it is evident that too much emotional reliance is consistent with increased neuroticism and fearful attachment, leading to the development of maladaptive behaviour.
